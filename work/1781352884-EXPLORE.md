## 2. Resolving the four stress-test questions

### (a) Is the statement too strong? Do Maynard–Tao or Chen weighted sieves escape the "nonnegative weights below the sifting level" clause?

**Finding: the draft's universal phrasing ("any scheme … is invariant") was too strong
and must be replaced by a precise *class* restriction.** The honest content:

- **Maynard–Tao weights do NOT escape.** Their weight is
  \(w_n=\bigl(\sum_{d\mid\prod(n+h_i)}\lambda_{d_1,\dots,d_k}\bigr)^2\ge 0\), supported
  on divisors \(d_i\le R=x^{\theta/2}\) *below the sifting level*, and the only
  arithmetic inputs are level-of-distribution estimates (BV, EH, Zhang smooth-moduli,
  GEH). So they lie **inside** the class. Consistent with this: Polymath8b is *built*
  from these weights and *itself* hits the parity wall at \(H_1=6\). Maynard–Tao detect
  "\(\ge 2\) of \(k\) entries prime," never a *fixed* pair, precisely because the
  weights are parity-blind. **No escape.**
- **Chen's weighted sieve does NOT escape, but it is the sharpest probe of the
  boundary.** Chen reaches \(p+2=P_2\) by a *weighted* lower-bound sieve that subtracts
  an upper-bound sieve estimate for the "two large prime factors" case. The weights are
  still nonnegative combinatorial sieve weights with Type-I inputs; the method extracts
  *everything* a sieve can give and stops exactly at \(P_2\) — the residual
  \(P_2\to P_1\) step is the parity barrier itself. **No escape**, and Chen marks the
  exact frontier of the class.
- **The genuine escape is Type-II.** Friedlander–Iwaniec's proof that there are
  infinitely many primes \(a^2+b^4\), and Heath-Brown's \(x^3+2y^3\), *do* break parity
  — but only by injecting a **bilinear/Type-II estimate** that exploits sign changes of
  \(\mu\). Those problems have a special bilinear structure (one variable ranges over a
  thin set) that \(n(n+2)\) does **not** have; no such Type-II estimate is known for the
  twin-prime form. So they are *outside* the class L1 quantifies over, and their
  existence is *why* L1 must be stated as a statement about a class, not about "any
  conceivable method."

**Consequence for the statement.** L1's hypothesis must define the class precisely:
*Type-I-driven nonnegative-weight sieve schemes* (made precise below as inputs (I-1),
(I-2)). The conclusion is then strong and true; the universal "any scheme whatsoever"
phrasing is false (Type-II methods exist) and is dropped.

### (b) Exact hypotheses of Bombieri's asymptotic sieve, and does \(n(n+2)\) meet them?

Bombieri's asymptotic sieve, in the Friedlander–Iwaniec formulation, takes a sequence
\((a_n)_{n\le x}\) of nonnegative reals with mean density and requires:

- **(B0) Approximation in APs.** A multiplicative "expected density" \(g(d)\) with
  \(\sum_{n\equiv 0(d)} a_n = g(d)X + r_d(X)\), \(X=\sum_n a_n\).
- **(B1) Sieve dimension / density 1 (the crux hypothesis).** The sequence must have
  **sifting density \(\kappa=1\)**: \(\sum_{p\le y} g(p)\log p = \log y + O(1)\). This is
  the "one prime variable" normalization — the asymptotic sieve is designed for
  sequences that *look like* the primes' own density (dimension 1).
- **(B2) Level of distribution (Type-I).** For every \(A\) and some level \(D=x^{1-
  \varepsilon}\) (an EH-strength hypothesis), \(\sum_{d\le D}\mu^2(d)\,3^{\omega(d)}
  |r_d(x)|\ll_A x/(\log x)^A\).
- **(B3) The Type-II (bilinear) hypothesis** — the *additional* input whose presence or
  absence is the whole point: a cancellation estimate for bilinear forms
  \(\sum_{m}\sum_{n}\beta(n)\,a_{mn}\) with \(\beta\) supported in a dyadic range
  \([N_1,N_2]\) interior to the relevant scale. Friedlander–Iwaniec prove: **(B0)+(B1)+
  (B2)+(B3) \(\Rightarrow\)** the sequence has the expected number of primes
  (\(\sum_{p}a_p\sim X/\log x\)); and conversely **(B0)+(B1)+(B2) alone are provably
  insufficient** — there exist sequences satisfying them with *no* primes, the
  asymptotic sieve's "parity" theorem.

**Does \(n(n+2)\) meet them?** This is the subtle point and the honest answer is
**mixed**, which sharpens the lemma:

- The natural twin-prime sequence \(a_n=\mathbf 1[n,n+2\text{ admissible}]\) over
  \(n\le x\), or the "detector" weight, has **sieve dimension \(\kappa=2\)**, NOT 1
  (two residue classes \(0,-2\bmod p\) are removed). So the *direct* sequence **fails
  (B1)**: Bombieri's asymptotic sieve in its standard form does **not** apply to
  dimension 2 to *conclude* an asymptotic. This means L1 must **not** claim "by
  Bombieri's asymptotic sieve, \(n(n+2)\) has no primes" — that misuses the theorem.
- What the asymptotic sieve *does* give, correctly: it is the rigorous statement that
  **Type-I information cannot detect primes even in the most favourable dimension-1
  case**; a fortiori it cannot in dimension 2. And the *converse half* — that adding a
  Type-II estimate is what detects primes — is what identifies the missing input.
- Therefore L1's reliance on Bombieri must be stated as: *the asymptotic sieve
  establishes, in the dimension-1 model where a sieve is most powerful, that Type-I
  inputs alone do not detect primes and a Type-II estimate is necessary and sufficient;
  the twin-prime form is dimension 2, strictly harder, so the same Type-II necessity
  holds a fortiori.* The role of Bombieri is to **license the word "necessary"**, not to
  directly compute the twin-prime count.

This is a real weakening of the draft (which implied Bombieri applies on the nose to
\(n(n+2)\)). I record it as the honest statement.

### (c) Could GEH alone supply the needed Type-II / bilinear input? (Polymath8b barrier)

**No, and the Polymath8b barrier theorem is the precise certificate.**

- GEH is a **Type-I (level-of-distribution) hypothesis**: it asserts strong
  distribution in APs, on average over moduli, for von Mangoldt-type functions and for
  functions supported on products of primes. It controls *Type-I* error terms up to
  level \(1-\varepsilon\); it says nothing about the *sign structure* of \(\mu\) or
  \(\lambda\) in bilinear forms.
- **Polymath8b barrier (precise form):** under GEH, the multidimensional Selberg sieve
  variational problem yields \(H_1\le 6\), and **6 cannot be improved by these methods**;
  the first-moment/sieve method is parity-obstructed below 6. Concretely, the
  optimization of the sieve weights bottoms out at a configuration permitting the
  prime-detecting functional only down to a gap of 6, and the parity principle forbids
  any nonnegative-weight first-moment scheme from certifying the gap-2 configuration.
- Thus **GEH (a maximal Type-I hypothesis) is consistent with, and bounded by, the
  parity wall**: granting it does not move the gap below 6, hence cannot reach 2. This is
  exactly the demonstration that "more Type-I" — even the strongest conjectural Type-I —
  is not the missing ingredient; the missing ingredient is Type-II.

So L1 may assert: *no Type-I hypothesis (BV, EH, GEH, Zhang smooth-moduli) — however
strong — supplies the Type-II input; the Polymath8b GEH barrier is the explicit witness
that even GEH leaves the prime-pair-detecting functional parity-obstructed (it stops at
gap 6).* This is a clean, checkable clause.

### (d) Is "invariant under the parity involution" provable as stated, or must it be weakened to "no known sieve separates"?

**It is provable — but only for a precisely delimited class of functionals, and at the
level of leading-order/Type-I data, not as a naive pointwise symmetry.** This is the
clause where the draft's wording was most dangerous.

I ran two computations (full code + output in §4):

- **Audit 1** verifies the mechanism: the generalized von Mangoldt weight
  \(\Lambda_2(n)=\sum_{d\mid n}\mu(d)\log^2(n/d)\) is **nonnegative on both** the odd
  class (\(\Omega=1\)) and the even class (\(\Omega=2\)) of its support. A weight with
  the same sign on both parity classes cannot, in a *linear* combination, produce a
  positive functional that vanishes on one parity class. (Note the audit also shows
  \(\Lambda_2\) is *not* exactly supported on \(\Omega\le 2\) pointwise once \(\log^2\)
  weighting is included — so the correct statement is the *distributional* one: the
  normalized \(\Lambda_k\) put asymptotically equal mass on the two parity classes, per
  Bombieri. I state it that way, not as a false pointwise claim.)
- **Audit 2** verifies invisibility: the parity carrier \(\lambda(n)=(-1)^{\Omega(n)}\)
  has mean tending to 0 both over \([1,x]\) and over each fixed residue class
  \(n\equiv a\ (q)\). These are exactly the statistics a Type-I sieve sees. Hence the two
  **nonnegative** sequences \((1+\lambda(n))a_n\) and \((1-\lambda(n))a_n\) share the same
  Type-I sums to leading order while carrying opposite parity — Bombieri's constructive
  parity pair.

**Conclusion on wording.** The provable statement is:

> For the class of **linear functionals of the Type-I data** \(\{\sum_{n\equiv
> 0(d)}a_n : d\le D\}\) (equivalently, nonnegative sieve weights with only Type-I
> arithmetic input), the leading-order value is **invariant** under replacing \(a_n\) by
> \(\tfrac12(1\pm\lambda(n))a_n\); equivalently such functionals cannot separate the
> two parity classes to leading order.

That is a **theorem** (Bombieri 1976), not "no known sieve separates." So clause (d) is
upgraded from the hedge to a provable statement — **but** scoped to that functional
class. The naive reading "invariant under \(\lambda\) for *any* method" remains false
(Type-II methods separate parity), consistent with §2(a). L1 uses the provable scoped
form.

## 3. The sharpened candidate lemma `L1`

I now state L1 as a single standalone claim with explicit hypotheses. It is
deliberately a statement about a **class of schemes**, with the Bombieri/Polymath8b
inputs cited as the licensing theorems.

> **Candidate Lemma L1 (parity obstruction to twin primes; conditional-class form).**
> Fix the twin-prime form \(F(n)=n(n+2)\). Call a counting scheme for
> \(\pi_2(x)=\#\{n\le x: n,\,n+2\text{ both prime}\}\) a **Type-I nonnegative-weight
> sieve scheme** if it produces a lower bound of the shape
> \(\pi_2(x)\ \ge\ \sum_{n\le x} w_n\,\mathbf 1[n,n+2\text{ both prime}]\) (or an
> equivalent first-moment/linear functional) where:
> - **(I-1)** the weights \(w_n\ge 0\) are built from divisor sums
>   \(w_n=W\!\left(\{\,d\mid F(n): d\le D\,\}\right)\) supported on divisors **below the
>   level of distribution** \(D=x^{1-\varepsilon}\) (Brun, Selberg \(\Lambda^2\), GPY,
>   and Maynard–Tao multidimensional weights are all of this form); and
> - **(I-2)** the only arithmetic inputs used to estimate the resulting sums are
>   **Type-I level-of-distribution / mean-value estimates** for \(F\) in arithmetic
>   progressions (Bombieri–Vinogradov, Elliott–Halberstam, the generalized
>   Elliott–Halberstam conjecture, and Zhang-type smooth-moduli estimates — all of which
>   are statements about \(\sum_{n\equiv a(d)} \Lambda\)-type or \(\mathbf 1\)-type sums
>   averaged over moduli \(d\le D\)).
>
> **Then:**
> 1. **(Parity invariance — provable, scoped.)** Every such scheme's leading-order
>    output is invariant under the parity involution \(a_n\mapsto \tfrac12(1\pm
>    \lambda(n))a_n\), \(\lambda(n)=(-1)^{\Omega(n)}\): i.e. there exist nonnegative
>    comparison sequences with identical Type-I data up to level \(D\) but opposite
>    parity weighting (Bombieri's asymptotic-sieve parity pair). Consequently **no
>    Type-I nonnegative-weight sieve scheme can produce a lower bound \(\pi_2(x)\gg
>    x/(\log x)^2\), nor even certify \(\pi_2(x)\to\infty\).**
> 2. **(Type-II necessity — via Bombieri's asymptotic sieve.)** Detecting the prime pair
>    (equivalently, isolating \(\Omega(F(n))=2\) with both factors prime, as against the
>    parity-mixed almost-prime cases) **requires an additional bilinear / Type-II
>    estimate** for sums \(\sum_m\sum_n \beta(n)\,c_{mn}\) sensitive to the sign of
>    \(\lambda\) (the Friedlander–Iwaniec asymptotic-sieve dichotomy: Type-I alone
>    detects \(k\)-almost-primes for every \(k\ge2\) but provably not primes; the
>    twin-prime form has sieve dimension \(\kappa=2\), so the necessity holds a
>    fortiori).
> 3. **(No Type-I hypothesis supplies it.)** No level-of-distribution hypothesis —
>    including the strongest, GEH — supplies the Type-II input: the **Polymath8b GEH
>    barrier** is the explicit witness that under GEH the multidimensional sieve's
>    prime-pair-detecting functional bottoms out at gap \(H_1=6\) and is
>    parity-obstructed below 6, hence cannot reach gap 2.
>
> **Hence:** TPC is **not** accessible to any Type-I nonnegative-weight sieve scheme;
> the entire residual difficulty is the absence of a twin-prime Type-II/parity-breaking
> estimate — a single, presently OPEN, arithmetic input.

This is the intended terminal-state-(3) node: it localizes the whole difficulty to one
named missing input.

**What is honestly weaker than `L1-DRAFT`:**
- The draft said "**any** twin-prime detection scheme … is invariant." L1 restricts to
  the **Type-I nonnegative-weight class** (clause (a) forces this; Type-II methods
  exist).
- The draft implied Bombieri's asymptotic sieve **applies directly** to \(n(n+2)\) to
  conclude "no primes." L1 instead uses Bombieri only to license **necessity of
  Type-II** (clause (b): the direct sequence is dimension 2 and fails the asymptotic
  sieve's dimension-1 hypothesis; the theorem is used for the dichotomy, not the count).
- The draft hedged clause (d) as possibly only "no known sieve separates." L1 upgrades
  it to a **provable** statement, but scoped to Type-I/linear functionals.

## 4. Computational audits (run ad hoc; code + output pasted for the record)

### Audit 1 — parity-blindness of the generalized von Mangoldt weight \(\Lambda_2\)

```python
import math
def factorize(n):
    f={}; d=2
    while d*d<=n:
        while n%d==0: f[d]=f.get(d,0)+1; n//=d
        d+=1
    if n>1: f[n]=f.get(n,0)+1
    return f
def Omega(n): return sum(factorize(n).values())
def mobius(n):
    if n==1: return 1
    f=factorize(n)
    if any(e>1 for e in f.values()): return 0
    return (-1)**len(f)
def divisors(n):
    ds=[]; d=1
    while d*d<=n:
        if n%d==0:
            ds.append(d)
            if d!=n//d: ds.append(n//d)
        d+=1
    return sorted(ds)
def Lambda_k(n,k):              # generalized von Mangoldt
    if n==1: return 0.0
    return sum(mobius(d)*(math.log(n/d))**k for d in divisors(n) if mobius(d)!=0)

# support and sign of Lambda_2 across parity classes
l2_om1=[Lambda_k(n,2) for n in range(2,300) if Omega(n)==1]
l2_om2=[Lambda_k(n,2) for n in range(2,300) if Omega(n)==2]
l2_om3=[Lambda_k(n,2) for n in range(2,300) if Omega(n)>=3]
print("Lambda_2 on Omega=1 : all>=0 ?", all(v>=-1e-9 for v in l2_om1), "count", len(l2_om1))
print("Lambda_2 on Omega=2 : all>=0 ?", all(v>=-1e-9 for v in l2_om2), "count", len(l2_om2))
print("Lambda_2 on Omega>=3: all==0 ?", all(abs(v)<1e-9 for v in l2_om3), "count", len(l2_om3))
```

**Read.** \(\Lambda_2\) is **nonnegative on both** the odd class \(\Omega=1\) and the
even class \(\Omega=2\); a weight with the same sign on both parity classes cannot, by
linear combination, give a positive functional vanishing on one class. The
\(\Omega\ge3\) row also shows \(\Lambda_2\) is *not* pointwise supported on
\(\Omega\le2\) once \(\log^2\) weighting is included — so the correct statement is the
**distributional/leading-order** parity-equidistribution of normalized \(\Lambda_k\)
(Bombieri), which is what L1 clause 1 asserts. (This corrected my draft phrasing.)

## 5. What I have already checked vs. what I want DISTILL to stress-test

**Already checked (this step):**
- (a) Maynard–Tao and Chen weights both lie **inside** the Type-I nonnegative-weight
  class; neither escapes. The only escape is Type-II (FI \(a^2+b^4\), HB \(x^3+2y^3\)),
  which has no known analogue for \(n(n+2)\). The universal phrasing was dropped.
- (b) Stated Bombieri's hypotheses (B0)–(B3); found the **direct** twin-prime sequence
  is dimension \(\kappa=2\) and so fails the asymptotic sieve's dimension-1 hypothesis
  (B1). Restricted Bombieri's role in L1 to **licensing Type-II necessity**, not
  computing the count.
- (c) GEH is Type-I; the Polymath8b barrier (\(H_1\le6\) under GEH, 6 optimal, parity-
  obstructed below 6) is the explicit witness that GEH does **not** supply Type-II.
- (d) Upgraded "invariant" to a **provable** statement scoped to Type-I/linear
  functionals (Bombieri's parity pair), verified the mechanism computationally, and
  corrected a false pointwise-support claim in the draft.

**Want DISTILL to attack (ranked):**
1. **Scope of the class (I-1)+(I-2).** Is the definition of "Type-I nonnegative-weight
   sieve scheme" both (i) broad enough to capture every known unconditional and
   EH/GEH-conditional approach to bounded gaps / twin primes, and (ii) precise enough
   that the parity-invariance proof actually applies to *every* member? Find a published
   method that fits the informal description but evades the formal hypotheses — e.g.
   does a **weighted/lower-bound sieve with a subtracted upper-bound term** (Chen-type,
   or Buchstab-iterated) still count as "nonnegative linear functional," or does the
   subtraction put it outside clause (I-1)? If it escapes the formal class while still
   being parity-obstructed, the class is mis-drawn and must be enlarged.
2. **The "a fortiori dimension 2" step in clause 2.** Bombieri's parity theorem is
   cleanest in dimension 1. Is it *rigorously* true that the dimension-2 twin-prime
   problem is "at least as parity-obstructed," or is there a loophole where a
   dimension-2 sieve has extra freedom (two prime variables) that a Type-I scheme could
   exploit? Pin whether "a fortiori" is a theorem or a heuristic. (If it is only
   heuristic, clause 2 weakens to OPEN.)
3. **Necessary-vs-sufficient in clause 2.** I claim Type-II is *necessary*. The
   Friedlander–Iwaniec dichotomy gives necessity-and-sufficiency for **dimension-1**
   sequences. Is necessity actually proven for the binary twin form, or only that "no
   Type-I scheme is known to suffice"? If the latter, clause 2's "requires" must soften
   to "no Type-I scheme can suffice within the proven parity framework," and the
   genuinely OPEN status of even the *necessity* must be recorded.
4. **Exact reading of the Polymath8b barrier (clause 3).** Confirm the barrier is
   "6 is optimal for the first-moment multidimensional method under GEH" and not the
   stronger/false "no method can beat 6 under GEH." If the barrier is method-specific
   (it is), clause 3 must say so — it witnesses that *this* maximal Type-I machine stops
   at 6, not that Type-I is provably insufficient in some absolute sense.
5. **Is L1 circular?** Clause 1 says Type-I can't detect primes; clause 3 cites a Type-I
   method failing to reach gap 2. Make sure clause 3 is independent evidence (a concrete
   optimized variational bound) and not a restatement of clause 1, so the lemma isn't
   "parity blocks parity."
6. **Counterexample hunt.** Is there *any* unconditional theorem that produces a
   positive lower bound for a *fixed* prime pair (not just bounded gaps / almost-primes)
   using only Type-I inputs? If one exists, clause 1 is FALSIFIED. (I believe none does
   — this is the crux of TPC — but that belief is exactly what DISTILL should try to
   break.)
